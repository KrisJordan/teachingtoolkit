# Canvas Discussion Summaries

_Last Updated: June 2026_

| Applications | UNC Supported | Cost | Time to Setup | Shortcut | FERPA Data |
|-------|---------------|------|------|---------------|-----------------|
| Discussion Summaries | Yes | Free with UNC Canvas | 2 - 5 minutes | [canvas.unc.edu](https://canvas.unc.edu) | [Yes<br>Use Inside Canvas](data-privacy.md){ title="Canvas is a supported course system for student discussion activity. Keep identifiable discussion data inside Canvas unless another approved FERPA workflow is documented." } |

Canvas Discussion Summaries is an AI feature inside UNC Canvas that helps instructors review discussion activity by generating a summary of posts in a Canvas discussion. It can be useful when you need a quick read on common themes, recurring questions, points of confusion, or conversations worth bringing back into class.

Use it as a first pass over a course discussion, not as the final account of what students said. A generated summary can miss nuance, flatten disagreement, or make a small pattern sound larger than it is. When you are grading, responding to a student, or making a teaching decision that depends on individual work, read the relevant posts yourself.

!!! info "Canvas Discussion Summaries is off by default"

    [UNC Educational Technologies](https://edtech.unc.edu/2025/08/new-in-canvas-discussion-summaries/) notes that Discussion Summaries must be enabled separately in each Canvas course. See the instructions below for enabling it in your course.

## Access for Instructors

### Enable It in a Course

1. Open your course in [Canvas](https://canvas.unc.edu)
2. Go to **Settings**
3. Select **Feature Options**
4. Find **Discussion Summaries**
5. Change the red X to **Enabled**
6. Confirm that the green checkmark appears

### Generate a Summary

1. Open a course discussion after students have posted
2. Select **Open Summary**
3. Type an optional prompt, or leave the prompt blank for a general summary
4. Select **Summarize**
5. Review the generated summary against the discussion before using it

Students do not see Discussion Summaries, the feature is available only to instructor roles, such as Teachers and TAs.

## Key Features for Instructors

### Get Oriented Before Class or Grading

Discussion Summaries can generate a basic overview of a Canvas discussion without a custom prompt. This is helpful when you want a quick sense of what happened in a large asynchronous discussion before class, office hours, or grading.

Start with the summary to decide where to read next, then return to the actual posts for student-specific details, examples, and context.

### Ask Focused Pedagogical Questions

The prompt box lets you ask for a more targeted summary. For example, you might ask Canvas to identify:

- questions students are still asking about a concept
- confusing terms or ideas that appear across the thread
- examples or cases students connected to the reading
- points of disagreement that could anchor the next class conversation
- themes that connect this discussion to the next assignment

The strongest prompts keep the task narrow and pedagogical. Ask for patterns across the discussion rather than judgments about individual students.

### Notice Tone With Care

UNC's guidance says Discussion Summaries can identify key takeaways, themes, language, and tone. Tone summaries can be useful after an icebreaker, reading response, debate, or reflection activity where you want to understand the overall shape of the conversation.

Treat tone analysis as a cue for your own review, not as evidence of a student's intent, emotion, or character. If a thread seems tense, confusing, or unexpectedly quiet, read the posts directly before deciding how to respond.

## Teaching Use Cases

### Turn a Discussion Into a Class Opener

Before the next class meeting, generate a summary of the discussion and look for two or three themes to bring back to students. This helps show that their posts shaped the next conversation instead of disappearing into Canvas.

!!! success "60-second Idea: Turn a Discussion Into a Class Opener"

    After a reading discussion closes, ask for the most common questions students raised and one point of disagreement. Use the result to write the first five minutes of the next class, then verify the summary against the actual thread before presenting it.

### Find Common Misunderstandings

In a large course, a discussion thread can reveal patterns that are easy to miss one post at a time. Use a targeted prompt to look for misunderstandings, confusing terminology, or places where students are using examples in unexpected ways.

This is especially useful for low-stakes discussions where the instructional goal is formative: seeing what needs clarification before an exam, paper, lab, or project milestone.

### Support Course Team Review

In courses with TAs or multiple instructors, a summary can help the course team coordinate what to read and where to follow up. Keep the summary in Canvas or another approved course-team workflow, and avoid treating it as a participation record.

### Enter Long Threads More Easily

Discussion Summaries can reduce the friction of entering a long thread. Start with the summary to orient yourself, then read the posts that matter for your teaching decision.

This works best for discussions where students are building a shared conversation. It works less well when the thread contains many separate essay-style responses on similar topics.

## What It Does Not Do

Canvas Discussion Summaries does not:

- grade or score student posts
- reliably measure participation, effort, or engagement
- replace instructor or TA review of individual work
- preserve previous summaries for later viewing
- show summaries generated by other instructors
- summarize attached files or linked web pages
- create images or other media from the discussion

UNC's documentation also notes a limit of **25 summaries per user per day** across courses.

## Data Privacy

Canvas is a supported course system, and discussion posts often become FERPA-protected education records when they identify students and connect them to coursework, participation, feedback, or performance. Use Discussion Summaries inside Canvas for ordinary course review.

Do not copy identifiable student posts, private course discussion details, or generated summaries into external AI tools or personal accounts. If a teaching workflow truly requires AI analysis outside Canvas, use a tool and process explicitly documented for FERPA or Tier 2 data, and share only the minimum student information needed.

Generated summaries can still reveal information about students, especially in small classes or sensitive discussions. Review and edit summaries before sharing them with students, colleagues, or anyone outside the course team. When sharing a class-level theme back to students, remove names and details that point to individual students unless you have a clear instructional reason.

For this toolkit, treat Canvas Discussion Summaries as an in-Canvas course review feature, not as a general-purpose AI workspace.

## Cost and Access

Canvas Discussion Summaries is available at no additional cost in UNC Canvas courses. Instructors must enable it in each course where they want to use it.
